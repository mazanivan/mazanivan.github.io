AI vs CTF

June 3, 2026 · 7 min

Intro#

First off, I just want to say thanks to anyone who actually took the time to click on this and read it. It honestly means a lot.

One more thing before I start. These are just my personal opinions and thoughts. You don’t have to agree with me and that’s completely normal. Everyone is different and that’s how it should be.

How It Started vs. How It Feels Now#

I’ve been playing CTFs for a few years now. I’ve done a lot of different competitions and I always enjoyed them. But this year something changed. I started asking myself if it’s still worth it. Like, does it still give me the same feeling of joy and fulfillment that it used to?

These thoughts came to me during a CTF from Slovakia called CyberGame. It’s actually the same competition that got me into the CTF world in the first place, which is kind of funny. Back then CTFs were my way of learning new things in a fun way. I would get frustrated, make mistakes, learn from them, and actually enjoy the whole ride. The process was the point.

I think what made it so special back then was that you had no choice but to figure things out yourself. You sat there for hours, sometimes days, reading documentation, trying different approaches, failing over and over. And when you finally got that flag, the feeling was just different. It wasn’t about the points, it was about the fact that YOU did it. You cracked it with your own brain. That kind of satisfaction is hard to get anywhere else.

AI Is Taking Away the Struggle#

Nowadays people don’t really sit with a problem anymore. The second they get stuck they turn to AI because they know there’s a faster way to get the flag and feel that dopamine hit. Everyone wants to be on top of the scoreboard and everyone wants to be the best. And I get it, that’s just how our brains work. But here’s the thing. AI is killing the frustration. And that frustration, that feeling of being stuck, is what actually teaches us the most.

I’ve done it too, I’m not going to lie. I’ve dropped a zip file into an AI tool, it analyzed the code, and sometimes it just gave me the flag like it was nothing. I asked it to explain the steps but if I’m being real with myself, that knowledge was gone from my brain in like 30 minutes. I learned nothing. Why did I do it? Probably because I didn’t want to be the guy who fell behind.

It’s kind of weird when you think about it. We are slowly training ourselves to give up faster. The moment something feels hard, instead of pushing through, we just ask a machine to do the heavy lifting. And every time we do that, we lose a little bit of our ability to struggle, to persist, to actually figure things out. It’s like going to the gym and using machines that do half the work for you. You’re there, you’re putting in the time, but you’re not getting as much out of it as you could.

Another thing is that challenges these days are either super easy or extremely hard. At this year’s CyberGame there was a task that needed three hints from the creators and only two people solved it. The real challenge isn’t making hard tasks. It’s making tasks that are useful for the player, where you actually learn something, that are doable but also hard for AI to just solve instantly. I honestly feel for the people who create these competitions. How do you make something that GPT or Claude can’t eat in five seconds but a human with some effort can still crack? That’s a whole new problem that didn’t exist two years ago.

This Isn’t Just About CTFs#

And honestly, this goes way beyond CTFs. You can see it everywhere now. Students are graduating and they struggle to write anything without ChatGPT. Junior developers are entering the industry and they can’t debug basic code because Copilot always filled in the blanks. People are losing the habit of thinking deeply about anything because the moment a question appears, the answer is one prompt away.

It’s not that AI is evil or that we should ban it. That’s not what I’m saying. But there’s something happening that I think is worth paying attention to. We are slowly trading our ability to figure things out for the comfort of instant answers. And the tricky part is that it feels good. It feels productive. You don’t notice what you’re losing until one day you sit down to solve something on your own and you realize it’s way harder than it used to be. Not because the problem changed, but because you got out of practice.

I saw a quote once that stuck with me. It said something like “we are becoming tools of our tools.” I think about that a lot. When was the last time you sat with a problem for a while without reaching for your phone or opening a chat window? For most of us, including me, it’s been a minute. The patience for sitting in that uncomfortable space of not knowing is shrinking. And that uncomfortable space is exactly where actual learning happens.

So What Do We Do About It?#

I keep coming back to the same question. Do we even need to know how to solve these problems ourselves if AI is always going to be around? Are we learning real skills or just learning how to prompt better? Honestly I don’t have clear answers. But I do think there’s a big difference between using AI to help you understand something and using AI to skip the understanding part entirely.

Maybe the whole point of CTFs today is that everyone can take what they need. If someone truly wants to learn, they will learn. If someone just wants a prize, they’ll use AI and take the shortcut. Nobody is going to check whether you used AI or not. The only person who will ever know is you. And that’s exactly why it’s so easy to lie to ourselves about it.

But I think the real problem isn’t even AI itself. It’s the scoreboards and the prizes. When you put a leaderboard in front of people and attach rewards to it, the motivation shifts completely. Suddenly it’s not about learning anymore, it’s about winning. And when winning is the goal, people will take the fastest path. Right now, that fastest path is often AI.

If there was no leaderboard and no prizes, the only thing anyone could really walk away with would be knowledge. Nobody would care if you solved it faster or slower than someone else. What would matter is what you picked up along the way. So my take is this. Let’s normalize creating CTFs that aren’t about comparing ourselves or chasing rewards. Let the learning be the point.

I’m not saying this fixes everything. But if we keep going down this road where CTFs are just about who can use AI better or faster, then I don’t really know what we’re doing anymore.

Final Thoughts#

If you made it all the way here, thank you. It honestly means a lot that you took the time.

I wrote this because CTFs have been a big part of my learning journey and I don’t want to see that experience get watered down into something it was never meant to be. It’s okay to use AI. It’s okay to take shortcuts sometimes. But I think it’s worth asking ourselves every now and then whether we’re actually growing or just going through the motions.

At the end of the day, you’re the only one who knows if you really solved something or if you just watched a machine do it. And maybe that’s enough. Maybe the whole thing just comes down to being honest with yourself.
